A Family Chronicle

The House of Betts

Sixteen generations from the fields of England to the founding of a nation — and beyond.

1480
Present

Five hundred years ago, in the flat farmland of eastern England, a man named John Betts was born. This is what happened after.

I · English Origins
Kent & Suffolk, England
Gen I

John Betts

1480 – 1550
Elizabeth Wright

Born in Wortham, Suffolk. The earliest known ancestor of the Betts line. Father of Robert.

Wortham, Suffolk 70 years

His son left the flat open country of Suffolk and moved eighty miles south — to Kent, a prosperous county with a market town at its center and dense forest to the south.

Gen II

Robert Betts

1508 – 1572
Agnes Terye · m. 1564

Settled in Bearsted, Kent — the first confirmed Betts in the county that would shape this family for four generations. Married Agnes Terye. Father of Alexander. His move from Suffolk to Kent, roughly eighty miles, was a significant relocation in the sixteenth century, but Kent was prosperous and Maidstone was at the center of it.

Bearsted, Kent 64 years

His son moved deeper — from the open farmland near Maidstone to the edge of the Weald, where the chalk downs gave way to thick woodland and the villages kept to themselves.

Gen III

Alexander Betts

1550 – 1594
Joan Larkyn · m. 1575

Moved from Bearsted to West Peckham, on the edge of the Weald — the dense, rolling woodland of southern Kent, home to a thriving cloth trade. Nine children in nineteen years. Likely buried at the Church of St. Dunstan in West Peckham, a small Norman church that still stands. Father of John, who would marry into the heart of the Weald and set in motion everything that followed.

West Peckham, Kent 44 years

His son John married Mary Biggs of Cranbrook — a town twenty miles deeper into the forest, where the same families had been marrying each other for centuries. The edge of the Weald married its heart. Whatever they carried, known or unknown, was passed down to the next generation.

Gen IV

John Betts

1576 – 1620
Yeoman of West Peckham
Mary Biggs · m. c. 1600

Baptized at Bearsted, raised in West Peckham. In Tudor England, a yeoman was a man who owned or leased enough land to support his family, hired help at harvest, and could sign his own name — a step below the gentry but well above a common laborer. John's will confirms the standing: he left twenty pounds to each of his three sons and ten to his daughter — meaningful sums, roughly a skilled tradesman's annual wage apiece. He named his wife Mary as sole executrix and his brother Alexander as overseer of the estate. He knew what he had and he knew how to leave it. Four children — John, Mary, Robert, and Thomas. He died in August 1620, at forty-three. Mary remarried within the year to John Bridger of West Peckham, and raised the children in his household.

West Peckham, Kent 43 years

His youngest son was four when his father died — with twenty pounds left to his name and a Bible printed in 1591. At twenty-three, he carried both across the Atlantic and never looked back.

II · Puritan Settlement
Crossing to the New World
Gen V

Thomas Betts I

1618 – 1688
Immigrant Patriarch
Mary · m. c. 1644

Sailed from England to Connecticut c. 1639, carrying a Bible printed in 1591. Co-founded Guilford, then became one of Norwalk's first settlers in 1660, purchasing a home lot on France Street — on land the colonists had bought from the Norwalke people two decades before. Nine children. Given the honored "round seat" in the meeting house — the highest mark of respect the community could bestow. His widow Mary survived him by over thirty years.

Arrived Connecticut 1639 Founder of Norwalk Freeman 1664 Estate £146

His son would never know England. Born in Guilford, raised in Norwalk from the age of sixteen — the first Betts to call this coast home, not a destination. The Norwalke people who had given the river its name were already fading from it.

Gen VI

Thomas Betts II

1644 – 1717
Civic Leader & Assemblyman
Sarah Marvin · m. 1680

Born in Guilford, raised in Norwalk from the age of sixteen. Selectman of Norwalk and elected Deputy to the Connecticut General Assembly across multiple sessions (1692–1707) — carrying the town's interests sixty miles by horseback to Hartford. Grew the family estate to £661, more than four times his father's. Addressed as "Mr." — a title reserved for men of high standing.

CT General Assembly Selectman 1701 Estate £661

His son would be the first Betts born, married, and buried in Norwalk — and the one who built the mill that fed the town.

Gen VII

Thomas Betts III

1682 – 1761
Community Builder
Deborah

First Betts to live his entire life in Norwalk — born, married, and buried there. In 1709, the town granted Thomas, his brother John, and two partners the right to dam a creek and build a grist mill, on the condition they grind all the town's grain. The Betts family also operated a fulling mill nearby — processing wool cloth — and the area became known as Betts Brook. Expanded into Canaan Parish (later New Canaan).

Built Norwalk Grist Mill Betts Brook Fulling Mill Canaan Parish Pioneer 79 years

His son inherited a town that no longer needed building. The mill ground the grain. The fulling mill processed the cloth. The creek they dammed became Betts Brook. He farmed, raised six children, and watched the arguments about liberty grow louder every year.

Gen VIII

Thomas Betts IV

1717 – 1787
Colonial Farmer & Father of a Patriot
Elizabeth "Betty" Benedict · m. 1748

Born the year his grandfather Thomas II died — one Thomas leaving, another arriving. Married into the prominent Benedict family. The quietest of the four Thomases — he held no recorded public office, but farmed, attended meeting, and raised six children through the French & Indian War and the gathering storm of revolution. Survived the devastating British burning of Norwalk in July 1779, when Tryon's forces destroyed eighty homes, two churches, and nearly everything the town had built. Died in a free United States.

Survived Burning of Norwalk 1779 French & Indian War era

For five generations, they built the town — its mill, its church, its fields. On a single night in July 1779, the British burned it all. His youngest son was nineteen. He had been watching.

III · Revolution
From Colony to Nation
Gen IX

Hezekiah Betts

1760 – 1837
Revolutionary War Veteran
Grace Hanford · m. 1785

Enlisted in the Continental Army in March 1780, at nineteen, after watching his hometown burn the summer before. Served in the 3rd Connecticut Regiment under Colonel Samuel Blachley Webb. Rose from private to corporal to sergeant through three years of active duty. Marched four hundred miles to Virginia with Washington's army and fought in the night assault on Redoubt 10 at the Siege of Yorktown — October 14, 1781 — under Lt. Col. Alexander Hamilton. Bayonets only, muskets unloaded, on a moonless night. Wounded in the attack. Cornwallis surrendered five days later. His neighbors called him "Captain Betts" after the war — a title of respect, not rank, that stuck for two centuries. In 2024, the Norwalk DAR placed a corrected marker at his grave: "Sgt. Hezekiah Betts." Twelve children, two became ministers.

Siege of Yorktown 1781 Wounded in Action 3rd Connecticut Regiment 12 Children

His son was born into a town still rebuilding from the ashes. The war was over. The republic was new. In workshops along the river, a new generation began tinkering with steam, steel, and ink. He had no interest in farming the land his family had worked for five generations — he wanted to know how things worked.

IV · Industry & Innovation
The Age of Invention
Gen X

Henry Betts

1794 – 1881
Inventor & Industrial Chemist
Mary Ketchum · m. c. 1840

Born in Norwalk to Hezekiah and Grace. A restless inventor who worked across steam power, printing, steelmaking, and papermaking. Built a steam engine and launched a steamboat on the Norwalk River — the engine exploded at Oyster Shell Point. The boat was destroyed, but the instinct survived. Patented a duplex printing method in 1838 — printing both sides of a continuous roll — that contributed to the development of the rotary press. Developed early steelmaking processes and a method for making paper from straw. Moved to Troy, New York, where his son Edgar was building a career in the collar industry. Died there at eighty-six — the man who turned the Betts family from the soil to the laboratory.

Duplex Printing Patent 1838 Steelmaking Processes Paper from Straw Troy, New York

The inventor died in a city he barely recognized. His son had made it the collar capital of America — and married into the family that started it all.

Gen XI

Edgar Ketchum Betts

b. 1842
Industrialist · Troy, New York
Harriet Louisa Gardner

Left Norwalk at fourteen for Troy, New York, with nothing but a suit of clothes and a place in his uncle's dry goods store. Built himself into the senior partner of Earl & Wilson — the firm that made the Arrow collar and helped make Troy the collar capital of the world. Married Harriet Louisa Gardner, whose father Jefferson was the first man in Troy to put a sewing machine to a collar. Together they raised four children at Elm Mount, the Gardner family estate in Lansingburgh. Edgar served as vice president of the Union National Bank, president of the school board, and trustee of the Emma Willard School. They founded Troy's First Church of Christ, Scientist. When he died in 1908, four hundred Earl & Wilson workers boarded a special train of seven coaches, each wearing a white rose, and stood bareheaded as his family passed between them. Harriet — then sixty-two — carried on alone. She purchased a bankrupt sawmill in the North Carolina mountains and founded the Laurel River Logging Company, putting her eldest son in charge of operations. She outlived Edgar by twenty-nine years.

Earl & Wilson Co. Troy "Collar City" Industry Elm Mount, Lansingburgh

The collar factory held no interest for the eldest son. In a stone laboratory on his mother's property, he ran an electric current through dissolved metal — and changed an industry.

Gen XII

Anson Gardner Betts

1876 – 1976
Metallurgist & Scientist
Hattie Alice Ramsey

The firstborn, named for his mother's dead brother. Yale Class of 1897 (Sheffield Scientific School, Sigma Xi), Columbia MA in Chemistry 1898. Invented the Betts electrolytic process for refining lead — patented 1901 — a landmark in industrial metallurgy still in use worldwide and still in the Merriam-Webster Dictionary. Developed zinc extraction processes. Published Lead Refining by Electrolysis (1908), translated into German. Managed the Laurel River Logging Company in Madison County, North Carolina — a company town of 1,200 workers his mother financed — then operated a manganese mine in Massachusetts during WWII. Fellow of the American Association for the Advancement of Science. Married Hattie Alice Ramsey in 1918 — a North Carolina woman who followed his restless career across four states and raised seven children along the way. Died in Northampton, Massachusetts, at ninety-nine — ten weeks short of his hundredth birthday.

Betts Process — Lead Refining Yale & Columbia Zinc Electrolysis 1905 Laurel River Logging Co. AAAS Fellow 99 years

His son was born in the mountains where his father ran a logging company. He grew up moving — North Carolina, New York, Massachusetts, Florida — following his father's restless ventures. Then the war came, and the Navy sent him to sea.

V · Modern Service
Into the Twentieth Century
Gen XIII

Horace Gardner Betts

1921 – 2005
WWII Navy Veteran · USS MacKenzie
Gloria May Morton · m. 1950

Born on Armistice Day, 1921, in Hot Springs, North Carolina. Enlisted in the U.S. Navy and served aboard the USS MacKenzie (DD-614) — convoy escorts in the Atlantic, Operation Husky in Sicily, months at Anzio, and Operation Dragoon in Southern France. Four battle stars. He came home changed. What the war put inside him had no name yet — they called it "battle fatigue" then, and men were expected to carry it quietly. He studied physics, taught it, and then the weight of what he carried won out. He married Gloria May Morton and had one son — born deaf. Gloria passed in 1975 while Wayne Sr. was still a young man. The distance between a hearing father and a Deaf son, and the loss of the woman who bridged them, meant the stories of the family never carried forward. The Betts name continued. The Betts story went quiet. He died on Anson G. Betts Road in Marshall, North Carolina — his father's road, in the county where he was born.

Operation Husky — Sicily 1943 Operation Dragoon — France 1944 USS MacKenzie DD-614 Four Battle Stars

His son grew up in the same house, looked at the same photographs, sat at the same table. The history was all around him — in his father's medals, in the old Bible, in the names no one explained. But no one signed the words, and no one wrote them down. It was right there — visible, untranslated, nearly lost to him, waiting to be inherited.

VI · The Living Legacy
A Legacy Rediscovered
Gen XIV

Wayne Gardner Betts, Sr.

b. 1951
Master Printer · Devoted Father
Kathie Marie Potter

Born deaf in Pittsfield, Massachusetts. Raised in a hearing household where he did not learn sign language until high school, when he attended the Austine School for the Deaf in Vermont. Went on to NTID at RIT in the printing trades. Spent more than twenty years as a printer — an unwitting heir to Henry Betts, who contributed to the invention of the rotary press four generations prior. Both of his sons were born deaf — a pattern that suggested something deeper than chance, something carried quietly through the generations. He and Kathie devoted everything to their sons' education — moving from state to state for the right school, from the American School for the Deaf in Connecticut to Frank Turk's North Carolina School for the Deaf to Austine and back to ASD, where both sons graduated. They sent them to Deaf camps across the country every summer and bought them a first Macintosh computer when no one else they knew had one. Every move, every dollar, every decision was shaped by one question: what gives these boys the best chance. When the printing industry gave way to the digital age, he turned that same devotion entirely to family. Settled in Rochester, New York.

NTID / RIT 20+ Years in Printing Austine School for the Deaf Rochester, NY

Their son would go looking. A middle name — Gardner — became a thread. The thread led back four centuries, across an ocean, into the villages of Kent where it all began. The history had always been there, waiting for someone to look up and find it.

Gen XV
First Son

Wayne Gardner Betts, Jr.

b. 1981
Filmmaker · Entrepreneur · Technologist
Sophie Sok

Born deaf in Northampton, Massachusetts. Studied filmmaking at Gallaudet University, then transferred to RIT — the same institution his father attended a generation before. Left in his final year to pursue career and entrepreneurship. Cut his teeth at Hands On VRS in California, one of the pioneering Video Relay Service companies of its era. Co-founded film production company Mosdeux, then convo.io — an on-demand sign language interpreter platform now serving over 20+ million conversations across 5 countries — where he remains the last standing co-founder. Most recently co-founded lexi.me, pioneering AI rooted in gestural and spatial human expression. Together with Sophie, he traced the Betts lineage — a history his father never knew — and found five centuries waiting. This site is the record of that search. Lives in White Plains, New York.

Co-founder, convo.io Co-founder, lexi.me Gallaudet University RIT Filmmaking Mosdeux Productions White Plains, NY

UI/UX Designer · Product Craftsman
Heather Smith

Faith · Fortitude · Service · Family

From a Puritan settler's prayer in 1639 to a sergeant's charge at Yorktown, from a chemist's laboratory to a destroyer in the Atlantic — the Betts family wove itself into the fabric of American history across five centuries. The story was nearly lost. A Deaf son in a hearing household, a mother gone too soon, stories that passed through the air in a language no one translated. Then Wayne Jr. and Sophie went looking — and found everything.
